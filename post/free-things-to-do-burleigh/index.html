Burleigh Heads is a suburb of the Gold Coast in Queensland, Australia — roughly an hour south of Brisbane by car and about 25 minutes north of the New South Wales border. It is not Surfers Paradise. It is quieter, more local, more real. The headland is a national park. The beach runs long and wide with one of the most-watched surf breaks on the east coast. On any given morning you will find locals doing yoga on the grass, kids eating fish and chips on the foreshore lawn, and surfers paddling out through the rocks like they have done it ten thousand times before.

The Headland Walk

This is the one thing you cannot skip. Burleigh Heads National Park sits on the southern end of the beach, and the coastal walk takes you around the rocky point through subtropical rainforest, past ocean platforms and over clifftops with views that people fly here specifically to see.

The full loop takes around 45 minutes at a relaxed pace — though most people stop a lot longer than that. You are walking through an actual national park. Pandanus palms, strangler figs, rainbow lorikeets, the whole thing. The Coral Sea is on one side, and the Gold Coast skyline builds on the other as you go. No entry fee. No booking. You just walk in off the beach.

The surf break at the base of the headland is worth watching even if you have never surfed in your life. Burleigh is a point break — waves wrap around the headland in long, clean lines. It makes sense when you see it. There are flat rocks above the break where people sit for an hour and don't even realise it.

Mick Schamburg Park & the Lookout

Walk five minutes north from the main beach and you will find Mick Schamburg Park — a grassy headland reserve with a timber lookout deck that juts out over the water. It is one of the better sunset spots on the Gold Coast. No crowds, no entry fee, just uninterrupted ocean in front of you and the city lights starting to come on behind.

Miami Hill lookout is just around the corner and slightly higher. On a clear afternoon you can see the full stretch of coastline from Coolangatta in the south all the way to Main Beach in the north. A lot of visitors don't know it exists. That's precisely why it's worth going.

John Laws Park & the Free BBQs

Just north of the Burleigh surf club, John Laws Park runs along the foreshore with free electric BBQs, pandanus shade, picnic tables and lawn that goes right down to the sand. It fills up on weekends because the locals know a good thing. Families arrive early, set up near the BBQs, and the afternoon stretches out much longer than anyone planned.

The BBQs are genuinely free — no coin slot, no time limit. Grab something from the butcher or supermarket and cook it here. If you time it right you'll catch the offshore breeze keeping things cool, the surf within earshot, and a sky that turns orange and red just as the chips run out.

Tallebudgera Creek

About two kilometres south of Burleigh Beach, Tallebudgera Creek meets the ocean at a wide, shallow sandbar that catches visitors off guard every single time. The creek flows warm and clear from the hinterland behind it, and at low tide the sandbar practically invites you in — calm enough for toddlers, warm enough to stay in all afternoon.

In summer it gets busy. On a quiet weekday morning it feels like a secret. There is nothing to pay and nothing to book. Just park up, walk down, and get in the water.

Walk, Run or Ride the Foreshore Path

A shared path runs the length of the Burleigh foreshore from Tallebudgera Creek in the south up past the headland toward Miami in the north. It is flat. It is right next to the beach. In the morning it belongs to the runners and the cyclists. By mid-morning the walkers take over. By late afternoon the people sitting on the grass outnumber everyone.

The full stretch takes around 30 to 40 minutes on foot at a relaxed pace, and there is nothing stopping you from sitting down on the headland lawn whenever the view demands it. If you want to hire a bike and do it that way, hire shops are easy enough to find on James Street.

James Street & the Coffee Scene

Burleigh's main strip runs parallel to the beach and has the kind of café culture that puts most cities to shame. James Street — and the lanes off it — is a mix of specialty coffee, juice bars, surf shops, small restaurants and people who dress like the beach is just around the corner because it is. Walking it costs nothing.

Sitting outside with a coffee and watching Burleigh go about its morning is, without exaggeration, one of the better free experiences in southeast Queensland. The coffee is excellent even by Australian standards, which is already a high bar.

Bleach* Festival — If the Timing Works

Bleach* Festival is the Gold Coast's annual outdoor arts festival, and Burleigh Heads is one of its main venues. Free performances, public art installations and community events take over the foreshore and surrounding parks for a few weeks — usually around the middle of the year. The programming is different every time and a lot of it is specifically designed to be free and outdoors.

If your visit overlaps with it, it is genuinely worth planning around. Check the official Bleach* website for current dates before you travel.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

What visitors most often ask about Burleigh Heads before they arrive.

How far is the Burleigh Heads headland walk from Burleigh Gold Coast Motel?

Is Tallebudgera Creek safe for toddlers and young children?
Yes. At low tide the sandbar is very shallow and the water is calm — far more sheltered than the open ocean. It is one of the best spots on the Gold Coast for families with very young kids. It is about a 2km drive south of Burleigh Beach, or around 5 minutes by car.
When does Bleach* Festival run?
Bleach* Festival typically runs in the middle of the year — usually June to August, though dates shift annually. Many events are specifically free and outdoors. Check the official Bleach* website for current dates and programming before you travel.
What is the best time of day to do the Burleigh headland walk?
Early morning is best — quieter trails, cooler temperatures and the light is incredible over the water. Sunset is a close second for the views. Midday in summer can be very hot as parts of the trail are fully exposed to sun.
Can I hire a bike near the motel to ride the foreshore path?
Bike hire shops operate on and around James Street, Burleigh Heads — about a 10-minute ride or 15-minute walk from the motel. The foreshore path is flat the whole way and suitable for all ages and fitness levels.
